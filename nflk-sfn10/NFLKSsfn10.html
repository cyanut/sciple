We are all superstitious...

..and the SfN (society for neuroscience) meeting may be the best place to share our superstitious rituals

Whenever I begin a new experiment or a new project, I do something stupid (...no, I am not talking about messing up with dilutions or experimental groups assignment ... that's routine) I am talking about something even stupider:
I open a random page of a random book, if the pagenumber on the left page contains the number 3, then I'll have good chances to publish my new project on a high impact factor journal.

why? ...well, because I am a scientist, I believe in magic, I believe in extrasensorial perceptions and I believe in chance controlling my life...

..and I know that I am not alone. Superstitious behaviors are a fundamental part of scientists' day-to-day job. Just ask your colleagues, you'll be surprised by the plethora of irrational behaviors that haunt the 'sharp-rigid-cold-mechanistic' brain of a (neuro)scientist. You are not alone! The SfN meeting is a good place to find traces of this dark side of our rational minds.

if you type in the word 'aura' in the visualization above, you'll see that the proof of existence of auras has generated quite a bit of excitement in the #sfn10 twitter community. The original tweet was re-tweeted almost instantly creating a circular pattern on the spiral (!hint: try to type in the word aura in the SEARCH field above...doesn't look like an actual aura? ... scary!).

So it is not surprising that when you look for the first 19 numbers in the SfN10 tweets the only number missing from this distribution is ... well, see the plot here below!


DAYLY UPDATES : Tweet volume during the conference days

We collected (out in the wild) some 3k tweets with the #SfN10 hashtag. The volume of tweets per day changes considerably across days, with monday on the top (936 tweets)!
We then found the top words in these tweets. Glenn Close seemed to generate consensus in the twitter crowd with 20% of tweets tweeting (and RT) about Glenn!
On the second day... again the tweet about the "spooky aura"!!
 The volume of the selected keywords changes considerably across days (with AURA showing a big peak on day 2 at the expenses of "more" scientific topics... BRAIN, TALK)
The Glenn Close effect is definitevly  more robust than AURA, persisting on the second day and emerging again on Thursday! ... glad to see that POSTER is pretty consistent across days!
